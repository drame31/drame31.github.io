QueuePilot — Featured Project

No line. No waiting room chaos.

QueuePilot gives small businesses a real-time virtual queue. Customers join from a QR code, watch their position live, and show up when it's their turn. No app download. No crowded lobby.

Built for barbershops, clinics, dental offices, and any business where wait time kills the experience.

The Problem & Solution

A problem that costs real customers every week.

The problem

Most small businesses still manage their queue with a notebook, a number ticket, or someone shouting names across the room. Customers don't know how long they'll wait. They leave. They come back at the wrong time. Staff spend half their day answering "how much longer?" instead of doing actual work. That friction is quiet but constant.

The solution

QueuePilot replaces that friction with a QR code on the wall. Customers scan, join the queue from their phone browser, and see exactly where they stand. When it's their turn, they know. Staff stay focused. Owners see the data. The whole system updates live — no polling, no refresh, no guessing.

For customers

Scan the QR code at the door. Add your name and what you need. Your phone shows your position and how long you're likely waiting. You can sit outside, run an errand, or stay — your choice. When you're next, the screen updates. No ticket stub. No app.

For staff

The staff panel shows the full queue at a glance. One button calls the next customer. Walk-ins can be added manually. The queue can be paused mid-session. Every action logs automatically, so there's always a clear record of what happened and when.

For owners

The analytics panel shows what the day actually looked like: when it got busy, how long each service took on average, how many customers were served, and how queue load varied by hour. It's not a complex BI dashboard — it's the four numbers a business owner actually wants to see at the end of a shift.

      The hard parts

      What this project actually required

      This wasn't a tutorial project. A few things had to actually work.

      01

      WebSocket broadcast across three roles.

      Owner, staff, and customer each receive different data slices from the same live session. When staff calls the next customer, all connected clients update instantly. Getting that broadcast logic right without race conditions took real debugging.

      02

      JWT auth with role-based access control.

      Three roles, three distinct views, all enforced at the API level. The frontend adapts its entire interface based on the decoded token — not just hiding buttons, but routing to different component trees.

      03

      QR check-in that works entirely in the phone browser.

      No app store. No install prompt. The customer view is a responsive web page served at a session-specific URL, accessible from any device that can scan a QR code.

      04

      Dynamic wait time estimation.

      The estimate recalculates on every queue change event, factoring in current position and average service time derived from completed sessions earlier that day. It's not position × fixed time — it updates as the queue moves.

      05

      Analytics built from real event data.

      Peak hour detection, average service time, customers served — all calculated from the actual event log, not hardcoded. The charts reflect what actually happened.

      06

      Walk-in handling that doesn't break queue order.

      Adding a customer mid-session recalculates positions for everyone already waiting and pushes the correct update to all connected clients. It was one of the trickier edge cases to get right.

      07

      Fully decoupled frontend and backend.

      The FastAPI backend exposes REST endpoints for auth and queue management, and a WebSocket endpoint for live updates. The React frontend consumes both. They could be deployed independently.

      08

      Built under real constraints.

      This was built while working full-time and studying. Every architectural decision had to be deliberate — there wasn't time to rebuild something that didn't work.

      Architecture

      How it's built

      QueuePilot runs on a FastAPI backend that handles REST endpoints for authentication and queue management, and a WebSocket endpoint that pushes live updates to all connected clients. The frontend is React 18 with TypeScript. When the queue changes — a customer joins, staff calls the next person, someone leaves — the backend broadcasts the new state and every view updates in under a second.

      Authentication uses JWT with three roles: owner, staff, and customer. Each role gets a different interface and different API permissions, enforced at the route level. The customer check-in flow is entirely browser-based — no app download, just a QR code that opens a session-specific URL.

      The data layer uses SQLAlchemy with a SQLite database. Queue events are persisted as they happen, which is what makes the analytics work: peak hours and average service time are calculated from actual event records, not estimates.
